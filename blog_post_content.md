# Planning Your First China Trip: The Itinerary Questions Everyone Asks (Keep Updating)

We've helped over 40 first-time visitors plan their China trips across Facebook and Reddit travel groups. The same questions keep coming up. Here are the honest answers, drawn from real conversations with real travelers. We'll keep updating this post as new common themes emerge.

## 🗺️ How Much Can You Really Do in 15 Days?

Almost always, travelers try to do too much. The most common mistake first-timers make is treating China like Europe, where you can hop between cities every day or two. China is vast, train journeys take longer than they look on a map, and the sheer scale of each city is exhausting in the best way.

Here's a rough guide that actually works. Beijing deserves 4 to 5 nights minimum—the Great Wall alone deserves a full day, and when you add the Forbidden City, Temple of Heaven, and hutong wandering, you'll fill every moment. Shanghai is more manageable in size but rewards slowing down with 3 to 4 nights. Xi'an needs 3 nights because the Terracotta Warriors are a full day experience you shouldn't short-change. Chengdu deserves 3 nights for pandas, Sichuan food, and Leshan Giant Buddha as a day trip. Chongqing is dramatic and worth 2 to 3 nights, though everything is vertical and physically tiring. Zhangjiajie needs 3 nights—two feels rushed, and the mountains reward taking your time.

The travelers who enjoy China most are the ones who do fewer cities better, not more cities faster.

## 🚄 The Golden Triangle vs. Adding a Fourth City

The Golden Triangle (Beijing, Shanghai, Xi'an) comes up constantly because it works. Three iconic cities, logical routing, well-connected by high speed train. For 10 to 14 days it is hard to beat.

But it depends on how much time you have. If you have under 10 days, the Golden Triangle gets squeezed. Beijing in 3 nights means rushing the Great Wall and skipping things you will regret. Consider a regional focus instead: Shanghai plus day trips to Suzhou and Hangzhou, or Xi'an anchoring a history-focused trip.

If you have 3 weeks or more, do the Golden Triangle and add a nature destination like Zhangjiajie, or a slower city like Chengdu or Chongqing.

A few routes we see work well: For 10 days, try Shanghai (4) + Xi'an (3) + Beijing (3). For 15 days, add Chengdu (3) and Chongqing (2) between Xi'an and Shanghai. For 3 weeks, add Zhangjiajie (3) routed between Chongqing and Beijing.

On getting between cities: high speed trains are almost always the right call over domestic flights once you factor in airport time. Beijing to Xi'an overnight sleeper is a classic. Xi'an to Shanghai in 6 hours on a day train is comfortable and scenic. Only consider flying for Zhangjiajie, where train connections can be slow.

## 🏯 Xi'an or Guangzhou? Keep Xi'an

We get this question a lot. Keep Xi'an, especially for a first trip.

The Terracotta Warriors are genuinely one of the most impressive things you will see anywhere in the world. The Muslim Quarter has some of the best street food in China. And Xi'an gives you that ancient China feeling that pairs perfectly with Beijing in a way Guangzhou simply doesn't.

Guangzhou is a great city but it skews modern and everyday. Save it for a second trip when you want to go deeper rather than broader.

## 🎆 Golden Week: Plan Around It

Bad enough to plan around. Most people don't find this out until it's too late to adjust.

China has several major holidays where domestic travel spikes significantly. Chinese New Year (January or February, date varies) is the biggest migration event in the world. Trains and flights book out weeks in advance. Many restaurants and shops close for days. Generally best to avoid China entirely during this window unless you specifically want to experience the festivities.

Qingming Festival (early April, date varies) is a 3-day holiday that spikes domestic travel noticeably. Less extreme than the others but worth knowing about, especially for train bookings.

Labour Day Golden Week (May 1 to 5) is a significant travel peak. Popular sites get crowded and trains fill up fast.

National Day Golden Week (Oct 1 to 7) is the busiest travel period after Chinese New Year. Trains sell out weeks in advance and major tourist sites reach genuinely overwhelming crowd levels.

If your trip overlaps with any of these, spend the holiday days in a large city rather than a scenic mountain area. Chongqing during Golden Week is far more manageable than Zhangjiajie. Book trains as early as possible, ideally the moment bookings open (usually 15 days in advance). And build buffer days into your itinerary so one delayed train doesn't collapse the whole plan.

## ☀️ Weather: The Thing You Should Research First

More than most people expect. Weather is the thing first-timers research last and should research first.

June through August is hot and humid across most of eastern China. Shanghai, Beijing, and Chongqing can all exceed 35°C. Not unpleasant if you're prepared, but exhausting if you're not. Front-load outdoor sightseeing in the mornings.

Zhangjiajie in spring and early summer is often misty and rainy. This is not necessarily bad—the mountains in mist are genuinely spectacular. But for clear blue sky views, October is more reliable.

Yunnan (Kunming, Dali, Lijiang) is the exception to the summer heat rule. Kunming sits at altitude and rarely exceeds 25°C even in June, earning its nickname Spring City. A good option if heat is a real concern.

October is widely considered the best month for China travel. Cooler temperatures, clearer skies, good visibility for mountains and landscapes. Just avoid the first week because of National Day Golden Week.

May is generally excellent but check your dates carefully. May 1 to 5 is Labour Day Golden Week and catches a lot of first-timers off guard.

## 👶 Traveling with a Toddler: More Manageable Than You Think

More manageable than most people expect, with a few honest caveats.

Shanghai and Beijing are the most stroller-friendly cities. Wide pavements, generally good metro accessibility, and most major tourist sites have paved paths. Chongqing is the trickiest because the city is extremely hilly and vertical, with a lot of stairs around the old city. Expect to fold and carry more there.

For food, China is actually great for young children. Plain steamed rice, congee, steamed egg, noodles, and dumplings are mild, soft, and available everywhere. Chengdu is the one city to watch, as the default cooking is spicy. Ask explicitly for "bu yao la" (no spice).

Timing matters too. Late October and November are excellent for family trips. The heat is gone, crowds are thinner, and the weather across Shanghai and Beijing is genuinely pleasant.

One practical note: a lightweight travel stroller you can carry for short stretches is better than a heavy pram. Bring a carrier as backup for uneven terrain.

## 🧘 How to Actually Relax Your Itinerary

Build in at least one full free day per week and don't schedule it.

The travelers who come back most satisfied are rarely the ones who ticked off the most cities. They're the ones who spent an unplanned afternoon wandering the French Concession, stumbled into Fuxing Park and watched locals do ballroom dancing, or found a hole-in-the-wall shengjianbao spot that they still think about years later.

The itinerary is a framework, not a contract.

## 🏙️ Beyond the Bund: What Shanghai Really Offers

More than most people give it credit for.

Fuxing Park on a weekend morning offers ballroom dancing, tai chi, card games, and zero tourists—pure local Shanghai. Wukang Road rewards lingering; the architecture is stunning and there are good independent cafes tucked into the old buildings. Shengjianbao from a street spot, not a restaurant, will ruin the fancy version for you permanently. Suzhou or Zhujiajiao as a day trip offers classical gardens and canals, under an hour from Shanghai by bullet train—a completely different pace. West Bund Art Center or Rockbund Art Museum are world class and rarely crowded if contemporary art is your thing.
